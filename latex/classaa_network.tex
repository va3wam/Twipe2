Take measurements of the Wi-\/\+Fi strength and return the average result. 

RSSI (Received Signal Strength Indicator) is a common measurement, but most Wi\+Fi adapter vendors handle it differently, as it isn\textquotesingle{}t standardized. Ultimately, the easiest and most consistent way to express signal strength is with d\+Bm, which stands for decibels relative to a milliwatt. Since RSSI is handled differently by most Wi\+Fi adapters, it\textquotesingle{}s usually converted to d\+Bm to make it consistent and human readable. mW -\/ milliwatts (1 mW = 0 d\+Bm) RSSI -\/ Received Signal Strength Indicator (usually 0-\/60 or 0-\/255) d\+Bm -\/ Decibels in relation to a milliwatt (usually -\/30 to -\/100). d\+Bm does not scale in a linear fashion like you\textquotesingle{}d expect, instead being logarithmic. That means that signal strength changes aren\textquotesingle{}t smooth and gradual. The Rule of 3s and 10s highlights the logarithmic nature of d\+Bm\+: 3 dB of loss = -\/3 dB = halves signal strength 3 dB of gain = +3 dB = doubles signal strength 10 dB of loss = -\/10 dB = 10 times less signal strength (0.\+1 mW = -\/10 d\+Bm, 0.\+01 mW = -\/20 d\+Bm, etc.) 10 dB of gain = +10 dB = 10 times more signal strength (0.\+00001 mW = -\/50 d\+Bm, 0.\+0001 mW = -\/40 d\+Bm, etc.)
